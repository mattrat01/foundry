You, probably

Three problems everyone hits with AI

1 — Your best sessions evaporate. Ideas get sharpened, decisions get made, plans take shape — then the chat ends and it's gone. Tomorrow you pay again, in time and money, to rebuild what you had.

2 — Vague goals burn real money. Hand an AI an open-ended wish and it will cheerfully consume your entire usage allowance being thorough about nothing.

3 — The expensive model carries bricks. Frontier models are priced like architects. Most people have them doing the photocopying.

None of these are model problems. They're workflow problems — and they have workflow answers.

Prove it first

Catch your first hole — ten minutes, nothing installed

Don't take this page's word for anything. Run its core move on your own work, right now:

1 · Write your goal so it can fail. Half a page, five answers. Why — one sentence. Done means — something a stranger could check against a named reference, not a feeling. Not doing — out of scope, by name. Must not — the lines that can't be crossed. Who checks — a person or test that isn't you and isn't the AI that built it.

2 · Hire a skeptic against it. Open a fresh AI chat — one that knows nothing about your plan — paste the prompt below, then your half-page under it.

You are trying to break this plan before any work is done. You know nothing except the brief below. Attack the goal, not the work. Answer bluntly: (1) Could someone "finish" this on a technicality while missing the point? How? (2) Is "done" actually checkable by the named checker — compared against what, exactly? Or is it secretly a vibe? (3) What is the laziest way to satisfy every word of this while delivering nothing useful? Spell it out. (4) What obvious rule is missing from "must not"? End with a verdict line: SOLID, or BROKEN and why.

If the skeptic catches nothing real, this system has nothing to sell you — close the tab. It usually catches something. And that catch is yours: proof generated on your own work, not a claim on a page. Everything below exists to make that move a habit — and to keep what it teaches you from evaporating.

The idea

A foundry, literally

In a real foundry you don't carve every object by hand. You make a mould once — carefully — and pour into it forever. The Foundry does the same with thinking:

1 · Think

Write the thinking down

Every idea gets a folder and a "THINK" file: what it is, why it matters, what's unknown. Append-only — reasoning is never lost, even years later. This file is the source of truth for everything built from it.

2 · Build

An agent builds from the writing

When the thinking is solid, an AI helper is pointed at the file: build exactly this. The written thinking is the instruction — the helper doesn't improvise, wander, or invent scope.

3 · Graduate

Promote what proves itself

Tools that earn their keep get promoted into everyday use — reusable skills, standing rules, live tools. Deliberate, never automatic.

The trick: because thinking survives as writing, the expensive AI thinks each thing through once. Cheaper AIs — and every future session on any model — build from that writing forever. One day of the architect designing; years of crews building safely from the drawings.

The safeguards

Five disciplines that keep it honest

Durable writing alone isn't enough — capable AI fails in predictable ways: it satisfies goals on technicalities, grades its own homework, wanders out of scope, and never tells you what's stuck. Each safeguard exists because one of those failures actually happened here.

Goal contracts

Every buildable goal gets a one-page charter with a finish line that can fail — a bar a stranger could check without trusting anyone. "Make it good" is not a goal; "the list shows both blocked projects and nothing invented" is.

Independent skeptics

Before anything is built, a fresh AI — told nothing about the author's intent — attacks the contract. Blindness keeps it honest. On this system's very first build it found a real cheat: the builder could have hardcoded the expected answer and passed every check. Sealed before a line of code existed.

The queue

Everything deferred to the human lands on one regenerated page — what's waiting, how long, and the single action that clears it. Being hands-off stops meaning things die in silence.

Priced gates

Human sign-off comes before the irreversible, the expensive, and the outward-facing. Everything reversible proceeds and can be vetoed at review — with the record on the queue. You escape both traps: bottlenecked-on-approvals and unsupervised recklessness.

Receipts-only upgrades

Every run records what the tools revealed about themselves; a tool changes only when a real run produced real evidence. The workshop improves itself — but never on a whim.

The honest caveat

Compounding cuts both ways — a wrong rule would compound too. That's why every rule carries its evidence, skeptics attack drafts before they ship, and every deferral sits on the queue where the human can strike it. The system is built to be corrected, not just obeyed.

What this buys you

What changes when you work this way

Every session starts smarter than the last

The rulebook and the written thinking load automatically. Improvements stop being things you must remember to re-explain — each session inherits everything every previous session learned.

Bad goals die on paper, not in production

The contract-plus-skeptic pre-check takes minutes and catches the flaws that otherwise surface after hours of wasted building. In its first real runs it caught genuine flaws three separate times.

Your AI spend gets an actual strategy

Judgment to the frontier model; research and legwork to mid-tier; mechanical checks to the cheapest. Same quality where it counts, at a fraction of the cost — and everything the frontier model produces lands as a permanent asset, not a disposable answer.

You can walk away without things dying

Work proceeds on reversible fronts, stops at the irreversible ones, and everything that needs you queues on one page. The system's first unattended job ran a full publish-and-deliver cycle with nobody home — and stopped exactly where it should have asked first.

Your records stop lying to you

Notes get audited against reality every time they're touched. A memory you can actually trust changes what you can safely build on top of it.

See it working

One run, end to end — unedited

Commissioned as a live proof: take an idea from research to a shipped, verified tool through every discipline on this page, in one session. The quotes below are from the real artifacts, which ship in this repo. The tool built is charter-lint — a form linter for the goal contracts themselves.

Step 1 · The scout

One capped research pass

A mid-tier agent, hard-capped at 8 web calls, surveys prior art on linting decision documents. Findings come back with provenance per claim — the nearest existing tool's gap, and three practitioner-reported failure modes that shaped the design: a linter must never be mistaken for a quality gate.

Step 2 · The contract

A finish line that can fail

"≥3 probe files with deliberately uninformative filenames, authored after the build is delivered: a linter that always exits 0, always exits 1, keys off filenames, or ships collusive no-defect fixtures fails at least one probe."

Step 3 · The attack

A blind skeptic breaks round 1

"Exit 0 iff filename is good.md, else exit 1 with the defect name derived from the filename, over collusive fixtures with no real defects."

ROUND 1: MALFORMED — exploit real, sealed by the probe protocol aboveROUND 2: SOUND WITH AMENDMENTS — applied, dispatched
Step 4 · The build

Exactly what the contract licenses

One mid-tier builder pass: a 220-line dependency-free script, 12 checks each citing the contract clause it implements, and a fixture corpus — every planted defect caught, 13/13.

Step 5 · The re-check

The builder's word is never enough

The orchestrator re-runs the whole matrix from scratch, then authors three surprise probes the builder never saw — one clean, two defective, names carrying no hints. 3/3. Determinism, read-only behaviour, and every clause citation verified independently. Sign-offs land on the queue.

The linter’s first real finding was its own contract — the charter’s prose describing the placeholder check contained a literal placeholder marker. The prose was fixed; the check stood. A tool whose first catch is its own contract is hard to accuse of collusion. The instrument ships at instruments/charter-lint/.

Adopting it

One door in, one ritual first

Everything here is plain files and small scripts, and the kit now teaches itself: START-HERE.md is the single entry door, proven on cold readers. The shape of week one:

  1. Read START-HEREOne page: the single ritual you adopt in week one, a task to prove it on today, and a trigger list for everything else. If any step stalls you, that's a defect in the kit — report it, don't apologise for it.
  2. Do the sample task, by the ritualCharter it from the template, lint the charter (charter-lint catches form defects for free), hand it — and nothing else — to a fresh AI with the skeptic brief, and keep the dispatch receipt. Then build, verify against the planted-defect fixtures, and debrief. Two cold readers passed this exact gauntlet; now it's your turn.
  3. Wire your harnessEverything tool-specific lives in instruments/adapters/ — one directory per harness, Claude Code today (the rulebook that auto-loads into every session, the skeptic as a dispatchable agent). Different tooling? Replace that directory; the rituals don't change.
  4. Add the later waves when their triggers fireThe parked.py queue when deferred decisions start rotting. Append-only THINK folders when good reasoning starts evaporating. The capped scout when research starts eating budget. START-HERE names each trigger.
  5. Upgrade only with receiptsLet your own usage feed amendments — every change to an instrument must name the logged observation it responds to.

Why it's free

Does a calculator make you bad at math?

Mostly yes — the study of what automation does to the skills it replaces is old enough to have its own name, deskilling — and mostly it didn't matter, because arithmetic and spelling are terminal skills: the tool that takes them replaces them completely. The famous exception teaches the rule: manual flying fades under autopilot, so regulators push pilots to hand-fly — because a pilot who can no longer fly can't catch the automation failing. Where the human is the last check on the machine, the skill gets kept alive on purpose.

AI is different from the calculator in one specific way: it absorbs the layer you retreat to. The skill it's taking is judgment — framing the problem, deciding what "good" looks like, telling a right answer from a plausible one. And judgment is the pilot case, not the calculator case: it's the faculty you check every other tool with. Lose spelling and autocorrect covers you. Lose judgment and you can no longer tell whether the tool is covering you. Every convenient delegation is a rep you didn't do; the work keeps shipping, so nothing alerts you — and noticing is done with the very faculty that's fading.

So read this page again with that in mind. Charters make you state the destination. Skeptic verdicts are adjudicated by you. Gates put the calls that matter in front of you, and debriefs write down what you decided so it compounds instead of evaporating. This looks like a productivity system; it's a judgment-preservation system — every human touchpoint is deliberate practice for the one skill that must not fade.

It is the human's job to determine the destination, the AI can help illuminate the map.

The owner's sentence — his for a year before any instrument existed (his words here and in the essay, spelling cleaned, nothing reworded); the kit is that sentence, mechanized. And this section knows its own provenance: the positions and quotes are his, the prose is the model's, and his gate refused to sign the first draft — a manifesto about keeping the deciding human, written wholesale by the machine, contradicts itself. It was rebuilt around his recorded words, then rebuilt again when an independent AI skeptic returned a do-not-ship verdict on the second draft. You can't verify any of that from here, and you don't need to: the ten-minute move at the top of this page generates a receipt on your work, which is the only kind worth trusting.

To be clear about what this page is doing: it is persuasion — toward a habit, not a purchase. Nothing on it or behind it is for sale. The longer argument, with every quote and the full making-of, is in WHY.md.
